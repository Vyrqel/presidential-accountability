Presidential Accountability
and the Law

Colin Moyer  ·  Government SS04151  ·  April 2026  ·  Website assistance by Claude

A constitutional amendment is required to eliminate presidential immunity and disqualify federal felons from holding office, because no person holding office should be above the law they are supposed to protect.

Evidence

Three pillars of the argument

01

The 2024 immunity ruling created an unprecedented legal class

Trump v. United States established a three-tier immunity system - absolute, presumptive, and none - without defining what separates an "official" from an "unofficial" act. This ambiguity grants the most powerful office the least accountability, contradicting the equal protection clause of the 14th Amendment.

Source: Delahunty & Yoo, Harvard Journal of Law & Public Policy, 2024
02

The Constitution does not bar convicted felons from the presidency

Federal statute 18 U.S.C. § 2071 disqualifies anyone convicted of mishandling classified files from federal office - yet constitutional requirements for the presidency mention only age, citizenship, and residency. Because presidential requirements are embedded in the Constitution, Congress cannot add restrictions without a formal amendment.

"Because the Constitution does not mention criminal records, a person indicted or convicted of a felony would not be barred from serving." - VOA News
Sources: VOA News; HowStuffWorks
03

State law already disqualifies felons from public office

Many states bar convicted felons from holding state office. The same conviction that ends a state political career leaves a person fully eligible for the presidency - the nation's most powerful position. If states have independently recognized criminal convictions as grounds for disqualification, the absence of a federal standard is a glaring inconsistency.

Source: Houston Law Review, 2024

Analysis

Who can be held accountable?

Every federal official - except the one with the most power - is subject to criminal prosecution for misconduct. The bar below represents relative authority; the tag shows criminal exposure.

"The President's accountability to the law is an integral part of the separation of powers and the rule of law." - ACLU amicus brief

Counterargument & Rebuttal

The case for some immunity - and its limits

Counterargument

Presidential immunity prevents fear-driven governance

Some degree of immunity may be reasonable: without protection, outgoing presidents could be targeted by their successors for purely political revenge - what the Court called "frightful despotism" and "alternate domination of one faction over another." A president who fears prosecution might avoid bold but necessary decisions.

"The Court hopes to spare the Republic from the 'frightful despotism' of 'alternate domination of one faction over another, sharpened by the spirit of revenge.'" - Delahunty & Yoo, Harvard JLPP
Rebuttal

Undefined limits make immunity a blank check

That concern only holds where immunity has clear, defined boundaries. The 2024 ruling rejected strong originalist evidence against presidential immunity and provided no workable standard for what counts as an "official act." Without clear limits, biased interpretation fills the gap - allowing serious misconduct to go unpunished. Presidents themselves historically acknowledged post-office prosecution risk; Gerald Ford considered a pardon for Nixon necessary precisely because prosecution was possible.

"... even presidents themselves have recognized that they can be prosecuted after leaving office. That is why President Gerald Ford deemed it necessary to pardon President Richard Nixon in 1974..." - ACLU

Conclusion

"This is not a feature of a democracy - it is a flaw in one."

The 14th Amendment guarantees equal protection - but equal protection is meaningless without equal application. The presidency reinforces that the president is a citizen subject to the Constitution's full authority. Article VI makes the Constitution supreme law, meaning any ruling that contradicts its equal-protection guarantee is on unstable legal ground. A constitutional amendment does not create new law; it makes the existing demands of the Constitution explicit and ensures no future ruling can contradict it.

A constitutional amendment eliminating presidential immunity and barring convicted federal felons from office is not radical - it is the natural consequence of applying the Constitution's own text consistently to the office it was written to govern.
